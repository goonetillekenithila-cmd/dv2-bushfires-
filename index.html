A continent that burns

Where Australians pay for fire — a story of disaster recovery, 2009 to 2026

Every bushfire in Australia leaves a financial scar. When fire damages homes, businesses, and infrastructure, the federal government activates Disaster Recovery Funding Arrangements to help communities rebuild. This is a map of who has paid the price.

01 — A continent that burns

Every Australian community hit by bushfire

Each shaded state shows how many bushfire activations of federal disaster recovery funding occurred in the selected year. Drag the slider to move through time — 2019's Black Summer lights up the eastern states, while the wet years of 2021 and 2022 leave the map almost bare.

02 — When bushfires strike

The seasonality and rhythm of fire

Bushfire activations cluster in the southern summer — October through February. Years like 2012, 2019, 2023, and 2025 saw spikes far beyond the baseline, while the wet La Niña years of 2020–2022 fell almost silent. Recent seasons suggest the pattern may be intensifying again.

03 — Where bushfires strike

The geography of Australian bushfire

Bushfire is not evenly shared across Australia. New South Wales bears the heaviest burden by a wide margin, with Queensland's exposure growing sharply in recent years. The small states see occasional but rarely concentrated activity. Note the shared vertical scale — it makes NSW's dominance unmistakable, at the cost of flattening the smaller states.

04 — The cost

The price communities pay

Disaster recovery funding has four tiers — from immediate personal hardship payments (Cat A) to exceptional recovery measures for catastrophic events (Cat D). Some local government areas have been activated more than a dozen times since 2009.

The ten hardest-hit local government areas

How often each recovery category is activated

A single activation can include multiple categories — Cat A and Cat B together cover the vast majority of activations, while Cat C and D recovery measures are reserved for the most severe events.

Severity of activations by state

Of the activations classified in the government data, A/B indicates personal hardship and essential works, while C/D indicates exceptional recovery for catastrophic events. Victoria stands out as the only state with more catastrophic (C/D) activations than routine ones — when fire hits there, it tends to hit hard. Just over half of all bushfire activation records carry a severity classification in the source data; the unclassified remainder is not shown here.

05 — The pattern

Trends, outliers, and what's next

The big picture: where are the heaviest burdens shifting over time, which single events shaped this decade, and where does the long arc of bushfire recovery actually point?

The ten widest-reaching bushfire events

Ranked by the number of LGAs affected. The 2013 state-wide NSW bushfires and the 2019 Black Summer reach more than fifty LGAs each — events on a continental scale. The January 2026 Victorian fires, only months old at the time of writing, already rank fourth.

The long arc: cumulative activations by state

Cumulative bushfire activations stacked by state. New South Wales drives the rise. The step-ups around 2013, 2019, and 2023 mark the worst fire seasons on record. Click any state in the legend to trace its contribution.

Bushfire's share of all Australian disasters

In some years bushfire dominates the disaster ledger; in others it almost vanishes. The 2019 Black Summer made up 55% of all disaster activations, while the wet La Niña years of 2021 and 2022 saw bushfire fall to under 1% — overtaken by floods.

Every bushfire event, by reach

Each circle is a single declared bushfire event, placed by year and sized by how many local government areas it affected. Most events are small and local; a handful, clustered in 2013 and 2019, stretch across dozens of communities at once.
